# THE WRIFTLANDS — Lore Bible

The single source of truth for all in-game lore. Every book in `src/lore_books.rs` — and every
smaller lore surface: well whispers, a dying knight's last words, a storyteller's tale, a
Choir hymn, a festival blessing — must agree with this document (or *deliberately* contradict
it — see **Bias & Contradiction**, and log the lie). When adding lore, check names/dates here
first; when inventing something new, add it here first.

---

## 1. Rules of the Canon

1. **This file outranks any book.** Books are written by in-world authors who can be wrong,
   biased, or lying. The bible records what is TRUE, and what each source *believes*.
2. **Secrets stay secret.** Section 10 tiers every reveal. A Tier-3 truth must never appear in
   a Tier-1 book. Foreshadow down, never spoil up.
3. **Style constraints (the font is law):** book text renders UPPERCASE, A–Z 0–9 and only
   `. , - + % : ; ' ! / ( ) ?` — **no quotation marks, no ampersand, no em-dashes** (use ` - `).
   Numbers are fine. Paragraphs are split on a blank line.
4. **Length:** books are 1–4 reader pages (a page ≈ 3 short paragraphs). Typical: 2–3 pages.
   Notes/letters can be 1 page. Nothing over 4.
5. **Tone map (locked):** warmth scales with civilization. Town books = warm, wry, storybook.
   Wilderness books = wistful, strange. Dungeon & Black Castle books = genuinely unsettling.
   Never gore for its own sake; dread over shock.
6. **Micro-lore obeys the bible too.** A whisper from a well, a merchant's patter, a knight's
   dying line, a tale by firelight — each is a lore surface with an in-world speaker, a tier
   ceiling, and a place in this document. Short does not mean exempt.

## 2. The World

**The Wriftlands** — the world's name in every age, named for the **Wriftheart**: a colossal
crystal heart that beat beneath the stone and held all lands in one embrace. Folk phrases:
*the Whole World* (nostalgic, pre-Sundering), *the Sundered Lands* (bitter, post). Formal
histories always say the Wriftlands.

- The heart did not merely hold the land together; its slow beat was the world's **weather,
  seasons, and tides of magic**. When it broke, all three went wild (storms grieve, seasons
  still turn but the skies are moody, and magic now *leaks* through the cracks).
- **Underground**, the world is honeycombed with caverns that predate everything — the
  **Starless Deep** — reached through cracked walls and cave mouths. The Deep is older than
  the heart's breaking and keeps its own secrets (Tier-4: never fully explained).
- **The wilds do not stay beaten.** Clear a raiders' camp and the ground lies quiet a
  half-season; then something else moves in — or nothing does. Folk truth: *the land forgets
  slower than men and forgives faster.* (This is the canon cover for re-tenanted rooms:
  the world is ALIVE, not scripted. Books may observe it; none may explain it.)

## 2b. The Kingdom of the Bright Line (backstory)

**The founding.** In the deep Whole Age, the first of the Bright Line — **Aldan**, a
shepherd-king in the stories, a warlord in Vell's drier telling — walked the land listening
for where the heart's beat came up strongest through the stone, and raised his hall on that
spot: the **Hearthstone**, around which the White City of **Cor Aldane** grew ("Aldan's
Heart"). The crown's compact, carved over the throne: *RULE AS THE HEART RULES - SLOWLY,
WARMLY, AND FOR ALL*. Nine generations kept it.

**What the kingdom was.** Not an empire — a keeping. The Bright Line built roads and bridges,
chartered the guilds (the Anglers and Provisioners charters bear the Line's seal — why the
guilds outlived the crown), set the four fairs into the calendar, and kept the peace so
lightly that most villages saw the crown once a generation. Their soldiers were few and
sworn hard: the royal levy's vow ran *until the crown gives us leave* — words that would
matter terribly (see the Unrelieved, §5).

**The white city.** Cor Aldane stood where the beat was loudest: white stone, bell-towers
tuned to the heart's rhythm, the Grey College's first hall, Maren's bee-gardens on the south
walls. Folk who visited said you could FEEL the city — a warmth under the feet, "like
standing on a sleeping hound." When the heart broke, the city fell into the wound in a
single night, and the Black Castle rose wearing its stones inside-out. (Tier-2: the castle's
halls still follow Cor Aldane's floor plan, wrongly — corridors that used to be gardens,
a throne room where the nursery stood.)

**The Line itself, in brief:** Aldan the Founder → seven keepers history mostly blurs
(Vell lists them; nobody quotes Vell) → **Azrel the Bright**, ninth and last, who built more
than all eight before him and broke everything. The Line has no heirs: Azrel and Maren's
only child died in the cradle, a year before Maren herself (Tier-2 — and the truest answer
to WHY her death unmade him: he had already buried the future once).

## 4. The Sundering — what actually happened

**The truth (Tier-3, assembled only across many books):**
Azrel did not strike the heart from ambition alone. After Maren died he could not stop asking
*where the dead go*, and Sorrel the court mage — half scholar, half flatterer — taught him the
old heresy: that the Wriftheart is not a heart but a **door**, and its beat is a knocking from
the far side. Azrel forged the Kingsplitter to open it and bring her back. The heart did not
open. It **broke**. The backlash dragged Azrel halfway through the wound and **fused him to
it** — king, blade, and the heart's stolen first-beat, all one thing now: the creature the
deep texts call **THE WRIFTHEART**. He guards the wound because he *is* the wound. He weeps
because some of him still knows.

**What the ten shards are:** the heart's body, scattered. Each sank into a land and took on
its nature (see the relic table — Verdant Tear, Heartwood Seed, Sunstone, Stormcore, Mire
Pearl, Gravebloom, Frostpetal, Emberseed, Sporecrown, Voidshard). Gathering all ten re-forms
the heart's body — and the body calls to its stolen beat. That is why the castle gate opens:
the thing inside *wants* the heart brought to it. (Tier-3 dread: the gate opening is not a
lock yielding. It is an invitation.)

**Killing the boss frees him.** The FINALE's mended world is also Azrel's release. The books
never say this plainly; the saddest ones let you work it out.

### Bias & Contradiction (the storytelling engine)
| Source | Their version | What they get wrong |
|---|---|---|
| Archivist Vell (*Chronicles*) | Ambition: a king who wanted more worlds to rule | Never learns of Maren's role; scorns "romantic" accounts |
| The Sharded Choir | The break was HOLY — one god became many pieces all may hold | Everything; but their inner texts contain real Tier-3 facts, twisted |
| Court letters (firsthand) | A grieving man, a whispering mage, a door | Fragmentary; no letter knows the whole |
| Children's rhymes | "The sky broke too, and it has never stopped grieving" | Nothing — the rhymes are accidentally the most accurate |
| The Grey College | A catastrophic "resonance event"; magic is a leak | Correct mechanics, blind to the human story |
| The Unrelieved | Duty without a kingdom: "we were not dismissed" | Nothing about the Sundering; everything about themselves — no knight will say the king's name |
| Roadside talk (wanderers, merchants) | Rumor, half-heard, self-serving | Everything and nothing; the false merchant lies for profit, the storyteller for beauty |

## 5. Cast (canon)

**The dead:**
- **King Azrel the Bright** — last of the Bright Line. Builder, then widower, then breaker.
  Now THE WRIFTHEART. In letters: precise, warm, increasingly obsessed.
- **Queen Maren** — beloved; died of the pale fever, 3 W.A. Kept bees; loved the bluebell
  fields (canon link: Bluebell Downs). Her garden is said to persist somewhere in the castle.
- **Sorrel** — court mage; see THE ANTAGONIST below. Officially: vanished in the Sundering.
  The Choir venerates him as First Singer (Tier-2: he founded them, or his writings did).
  The Grey College refuses to speak his name.
- **Torwen the Bold** (fl. 117 A.S.) — shard-seeker; took three, broke at the fourth, went
  home, lived quietly, died old. His journals romanticize nothing.

**The living (present day, 312 A.S.):**
- **Wren of Emberfall** — the OTHER survivor of the burning: she stayed. She stands vigil in
  the ashes, points travelers to the living towns, and wrote the survivor's plea (*The Last
  Night of Emberfall*). Weathered, kind, permanently done with running. She never learns who
  else escaped that night (the hero); if she suspects, she keeps it. First-hour quest-giver
  (THE LAST EMBER). Never a comic figure.
- **Archivist Vell** — dry, exact, secretly kind. Writes the *Chronicles*. Distrusts songs.
- **Geographer Mull** — walks everywhere, complains constantly, loves the world helplessly.
- **Warden Ilse** — hunts corrupted beasts; her folios insist monsters are *wounded, not evil*.
- **Goodwife Bren** — farmer-almanac author. No-nonsense; secretly writes the children's rhymes
  (Tier-1 wink, revealed in her diary).
- **Old Angler Pork, Forge-hand Dun, Peddler Coss, Apothecary Sage, Captain Maree** — trade
  authors; each gets a voice, not just facts (Pork lies about fish sizes; Dun grieves a
  brother lost to a mine in the Deep; Coss confesses small swindles).
- **The hero** — the one from Emberfall's last cottage, who fled the burning the night the game
  begins. Books never name them, never give their age (the creator lets the player be anyone),
  and can only mention Emberfall as FRESH news — it burned this year. If the player is named
  Baz, the Bald Hero ballads land as prophecy; write them to work either way.

**Factions:**
- **The Sharded Choir** — pro-rift cult. Outer face: serene pilgrims ("let it stay broken").
  Inner truth: they feed the wound. They caused Emberfall (Tier-3). Scripture escalates from
  soothing to skin-crawling across their five liturgies. Their moonlit rites and conclaves are
  met in the wilds at night; their processions walk the deep roads by day.
- **The Lantern Order** — grave-tenders. Kind, tired, unarmed. "We bury the dead to keep them
  still; since the Gravebloom fell, the stillness fails." They remember names so the dead don't.
- **The Grey College** — magic scholars. Founded under Azrel; survived him. Pompous, useful.
  Their primers double as gameplay manuals (wands, runes, mana).
- **The Unrelieved** — what is left of the Bright Line's last levy of knights. When the crown
  drowned with Cor Aldane, no authority survived to dismiss them, and their vow has no clause
  for a dead kingdom — so they patrol. Alone, ageing, unpaid for three centuries of service,
  they ride the wilds putting themselves between ordinary folk and the worst of the dark, and
  they die out there, one by one, giving their blades to whoever kneels beside them at the
  end. (Canon for every DYING KNIGHT met in the wild: his last tale is true; his order is
  "ashes" because the Unrelieved never recruit — you cannot join a vow that was sworn to a
  living king.) Blue-and-silver colors; a tilted banner marks where one made his stand.
  Tier-1: knights exist and are honored. Tier-2: who they were sworn to.
- **The town guilds** — practical, local, beloved: the **Anglers** and the **Provisioners**
  keep halls in the cities (many stand half-ruined since leaner years; restoring a hall wing
  by wing is a civic act the whole town notices). Guild charters are older than the towns
  that hold them — a Tier-2 crumb: the guilds predate the Scattering, which means they
  survived the Sundering as INSTITUTIONS when the kingdom itself did not.

## 5b. THE ANTAGONIST — Sorrel, the First Singer

**Who he was (Tier 2, assembled from court letters and College silences).** A foundling
taken in by the palace scholars, brilliant past everyone's comfort and promoted past no
one's. As a young surveyor he was sent to map cave systems for the crown and went too deep —
into the **Starless Deep** — and came back changed in the quiet way: he had found deep-folk
glyphs, and in them the old heresy: *the heart is a door, and its beat is a knocking.* The
College's precursors called it debris of a dead religion. Sorrel called it the only question
worth a life. He spent thirty years climbing to the king's ear to test it.

**What he did.** He did not corrupt Azrel with lies — that is what makes him unbearable. He
told a grieving man something he himself BELIEVED with his whole soul, at the exact moment
grief made belief irresistible. The forging of the Kingsplitter was Sorrel's design; the
striking was Azrel's hand; the wound belongs to them both.

**What he is now (Tier 3 — the deep truth).** The backlash that fused Azrel to the wound's
BODY unmade Sorrel's body entirely and fused what was left to the wound's VOICE. He is the
whisper. He is why the shards murmur to their carriers, why the Spire hums, why the wells
learned to talk back. "First Singer" is not a title — it is a job description: every hymn
the Choir sings, he taught them, one listener at a time across three centuries. Each
generation, the cultist who hears him clearest is raised up as **Choirmaster** — his hands
in the world (the Saltmaze boss is the current pair).

**What he wants.** The Sundering proved him HALF right — the heart broke instead of opening.
Three hundred years on the far side of his own mistake, he wants what he always wanted: the
door OPEN. His doctrine, fed through the Choir: **grief widens the wound.** Azrel's grief
broke the world; the sky's grief leaks through it as weather; so the Choir MANUFACTURES
grief — a village burned here, a harvest poisoned there — and sings the widening hymns over
the mourning. Emberfall was not spite. It was arithmetic (Tier-3: the Calling of the Ember,
a widening rite — the burning was the point, and the grieving after was the harvest).

**His plan, and how the game's shape serves it.** The gate of the Black Castle opens for a
re-formed heart — the INVITATION (§4) is his engineering: he needs the heart's body carried
to its stolen beat, and a hero gathering ten shards is doing his fetching. And he keeps the
Kingsplitter on an altar because the final verse needs it: when the door stands at last
ajar, THE FIRST BELL must ring a second time. (Which means the player's Saltmaze theft of
the blade — undertaken for a better sword — quietly forecloses his ending. No book says
this. Let the attentive feel the floor shift.)

**Why he loses.** Sorrel has never once understood WHY people grieve — only that they do,
and that it is useful. The Lantern Order remembering names, a fire ringed with strangers
telling tales, the towns holding their fairs in a broken world: every small warmth is
counter-magic he cannot model. The finale is Azrel's release and Sorrel's bereavement — the
voice loses the only other one who was THERE. Post-finale canon (if ever needed): the
whisper does not stop; it just, for the first time, has no plan.

**Voice, when he must be quoted (inner liturgy, deep texts only):** patient, warm, precise —
a teacher who has already forgiven you for being slow. Never shouts. Never lies outright;
he curates. The most chilling texts read as KINDNESS. Casual books know him only as a name
scholars won't say; folk know the whisper as "the quiet man in the wind" (Tier-1 ghost
story, accidentally true).

## 6. Gazetteer — the Four-and-Twenty Lands

Formal names used in histories (game biome key in parens). Each far land is shaped by its shard
or its distance from the heart.

**Heartlands (T0):** the Verdant Reach (grassland) · the Heartwood (forest) · Honeyglade ·
Bluebell Downs (Maren's flowers gone wild)
**Nearlands (T1):** the Golden Waste (desert) · the Stormcrown (mountains) · Petalwood ·
the Greenmaw (jungle that eats roads)
**Midlands (T2):** the Mire (swamp) · the White Silence (arctic) · the Prismwastes (light bends
wrong) · Suncoast (the drowned harbor — bells ring with no hands)
**Farlands (T3):** the Gravelands (graveyard) · the Dreaming Grove (mushroom) · Stormreach
(the sky's anger pools here) · the Tarmire
**Wounded lands (T4):** the Cinderwood (burnt) · the Hollowwood (fog that hides things) ·
Galewind Steppe · the Saltwastes
**The Broken rim (T5):** the Unmade (chaos — where the heart bled out) · the Embermaw ·
the Witherlands (rot that thinks) · the Blackdeep (no sky at all)

**Named places:**
- **Cor Aldane** — the White City, swallowed; the Black Castle wears its stones.
- **Emberfall** — the hero's village, burned 312 A.S.; the ruined home village in-game.
  Wren keeps its vigil.
- **The Starless Deep** — all underground; the deep-folk glyphs glow when you look away —
  Tier-4, never explained.
- **The Rift Spire** — ONE to a world: a black three-tiered tower out past the last maps,
  standing where the world's skin is thinnest. No chronicle records its building; the College
  insists it *accreted* ("the wound scabbing upward"); the Choir calls it the Second Bell and
  makes pilgrimage. Inside, the floors go DOWN forever — each deeper turn worse than the
  last, and no one has written home from the bottom. Folk name: **the Hum** (it hums at
  night; shepherds two lands away claim they feel it in their teeth). Tier-1: it exists, far
  away. Tier-2: it descends without end. Tier-3: it is not a building at all — it is the
  wound's echo given architecture, and the shards of the world that orbit its crown are
  pieces of the night the Sundering tore loose.
- **The Embermaw's molten fields** — the land still bleeding fire three centuries on. The
  Emberseed keeps it burning (Tier-2). Crossing the melt is possible and always costs; the
  fields bubble like a pot that never comes off the fire, and at night they are the only
  light for miles.
- **The towns** — unnamed in the bible; the game generates their names.

## 7. Cosmology & Magic

- **Magic is a leak.** Where the heart cracked the world, power seeps through; a wand is "a
  straw to sip it" (canon, *The First Wand*). Runes teach a wand to remember one spell.
- **Mana** is a person's capacity to hold the leak without harm. Rest refills it; the Deep
  strains it.
- **Weather is grief** (folk truth) / **weather is the heart's arrhythmia** (College truth).
  Both are the same fact. Storms cluster where shards lie (Stormreach). Rain waters crops —
  farmers pray for grey mornings.
- **THE MUSIC OF THE WORLD (the connective tissue — write toward it constantly):** the
  Wriftheart's beat was the world's first music, and everything musical descends from it.
  Songs played on a flute can still MOVE the world (open stone, call rain, match a hidden
  pitch) because a song is a *remembered fragment of the beat*. The Choir SINGS because
  singing is how you speak to the wound. Bells matter everywhere: Suncoast's drowned bells
  ring with no hands, Bellnight closes the year in bell-song, the Choir calls the
  Kingsplitter THE FIRST BELL, and the Rift Spire HUMS. A book that needs magic to feel holy
  or dreadful should reach for music before it reaches for light. (Tier-2 as pattern; the
  full claim — all music is the heart remembering itself — is Tier-3, and only the saddest
  texts say it.)
- **GRIEF FEEDS THE WOUND (the antagonist's physics, Tier-3):** the wound drinks sorrow.
  Azrel's grief broke it; weather is its slow leak; the Choir burns and poisons to brew more.
  The counter is not swords — it is remembering: the Lantern Order keeping names, fires at
  night, fairs held in a broken world. Books written warm ARE the resistance.
- **Corruption:** beasts near shards "wear a second shadow" — bigger, angrier, lit from within.
  Elites lead; champions rule. They are wounded, not evil (Ilse's hill to die on).
- **The dead:** rise only in the Gravelands' reach BY DAY — but the night belongs to them
  everywhere. See below.
- **THE NIGHT HAS TENANTS (canon for night events):** since the Sundering, night is not
  merely dark — it is *the wound's half of the day*. The restless dead walk under any sky
  after dusk; the Choir holds its rites by moonlight; pale things howl in the moors; and
  honest folk answer with FIRE — the story circle, the trapper's fire, the stargazers' camp.
  A lit fire at night is the oldest human argument against the dark, and the books should
  treat a ring of strangers around one as quietly sacred.
- **STARFALL & STAR-METAL:** the sky broke with the heart (the rhyme is literally true), and
  ever since, it sheds. Some nights a fleck of the old sky falls guarded by glimmering
  things; rarely, a whole CRATER of star-metal is found still warm — silver through
  voidsteel by how deep in the wounded lands it landed. Smiths prize it; the College wants
  it catalogued; the Choir teaches that each fall is the sky weeping a tooth. Tier-1: stars
  fall and can be mined. Tier-3: star-metal is night-sky torn loose at the Sundering, which
  is why voidsteel "drinks the light."
- **THE WHISPERING WELLS:** dug in the Whole Age over springs that touch the Starless Deep.
  The water remembers — toss a coin and it answers: mostly murmurs, sometimes a blessing or
  a small gift back, rarely a place shown true. And once in a very long while a well says a
  name that no one taught it. The College has theories (resonance, echo, leak). The Lantern
  Order forbids its members to listen. NEVER explain the wells fully — they sit at the edge
  of Tier-4, and the name-whisper is Tier-4 outright: books may report it happening; none
  may say how.
- **Seasonal truths (folk-observed, canon-true):** spring wakes the spores and the thorns
  ("growth runs wild — the land trying to heal too fast"); midsummer heat maddens the ember
  kind far from their fields; autumn hunger drives wolf and goblin alike toward the
  harvests; and in winter the cold creeps into every land — the White Silence's shard
  reaching farther while the world's blood runs slow.

## 8. Calendar & Festivals (canon)

Four 28-day seasons. The four fairs were set by royal decree in Azrel's golden years and the
towns have never let go of them — holding the fairs IS an act of remembering the Whole World.

| Season day 12 | Fair | What it is | Canon note |
|---|---|---|---|
| Spring | **THE SEED FAIR** | Seed gifts, planting luck | Oldest of the four; predates the crown (Tier-2: a Whole Age farmers' rite the decree merely adopted) |
| Summer | **THE GREAT CAST** | Fishing derby, double catch | Founded by the Anglers guild; Pork claims his grandmother won it "nine times, all cheating" |
| Fall | **THE HARVEST FAIR** | Crops fetch double | Bren's almanac calls it "the only argument that always wins: dinner" |
| Winter | **BELLNIGHT** | Bells at dusk; a blessing on all who stand in town | The one night a year every town rings together. Folk say the bells "answer something far away" — and on Bellnight, the Rift Spire's hum falls SILENT (Tier-2 crumb; no book says which listens to which — Tier-4) |

## 9. The Hero's Thread (Emberfall)

Canon of the personal arc, revealed across the four **Emberfall fragments**:
1. *The Last Night of Emberfall* (exists) — Wren's plea: "go and put the world back together."
2. A charred school slate — the village children's names; one line unfinished. (1 page, devastating, no author.)
3. The miller's unsent letter — suspicious strangers in grey robes camped on the hill that week (Tier-3 breadcrumb → the Choir).
4. A Lantern Order record — who they buried at Emberfall, and the one grave left empty *(the one they never found — the hero, who fled that night)*.

The FIRST-HOUR QUESTS carry the same thread in play: Wren's THE LAST EMBER (reach the living
town) and the keeper's THE FIRST SHARD (an axe, a pick, and a marked den). Any text touching
these must keep Wren's voice consistent with §5.

## 10. Secrets Ledger (reveal tiers)

- **Tier 1 — common knowledge:** the Sundering happened; ten shards; the castle is sealed;
  the far lands are warped; knights of a dead kingdom still ride; stars fall and are mined;
  a lone spire hums past the last maps. *Any book may state this.*
- **Tier 2 — uncommon:** Maren's death broke the king (and the cradle before her — the lost
  heir); Sorrel whispered the door-heresy and found it in the Deep; the castle wears Cor
  Aldane's floor plan wrongly; the
  Choir's founder connection; the shards whisper to carriers; the guild charters predate the
  Scattering; the Spire descends without end; Bellnight silences the Hum; the Emberseed keeps
  the Embermaw burning. *Court letters, Torwen, Choir outer texts, Lantern records, guild
  histories.*
- **Tier 3 — deep truths:** Azrel is FUSED with the wound (the boss IS the king); the gate
  opening is an invitation — and SORREL'S engineering; Sorrel lives as the wound's voice and
  leads the Choir as First Singer; grief widens the wound and Emberfall was a widening rite;
  the Kingsplitter is the final verse of his plan; Torwen's three shards were re-hidden; the
  Spire is the wound's echo, not a building; star-metal is the torn night sky; all music is
  the heart remembering itself. *Black Castle papers, Choir inner liturgy, the last Torwen
  journal — dungeon/castle finds only.*
- **Tier 4 — never answered:** what is on the far side of the door; what the Deep is; what
  the deep-folk were; whether Maren waits anywhere; how a well learns a name; what Bellnight's
  bells answer. *Books may ask. None may answer.*

## 12. The Kingsplitter (the master-sword questline — BUILT)

**Canon:** the blade Azrel forged to open the heart did not shatter with it. In the chaos of
the Sundering it was carried from the drowned city — and generations later the **Sharded
Choir** recovered it. To them it is the holiest object in the world: the instrument of the
break, which they call **THE FIRST BELL** ("it struck the heart and rang the world into
many"). They keep it on an altar at the bottom of their hidden sanctum: **the Saltmaze**, a
labyrinth of old salt-mine galleries beneath the **Saltwastes**, recut by the cult so only
those who know the hymns can walk it.

**The trail (books ARE the map — no quest marker):**
1. *Tier 1 — a ballad* hints the blade survived ("the sword that broke the sky was carried
   from the fire").
2. *Tier 2 — a Chronicle/court letter* records that "the singers took up the kingsblade" —
   pointing at the Choir.
3. *Tier 2/3 — a Torwen margin note or Choir liturgy* names the place: "the singers beneath
   the salt." → the Saltwastes hide an entrance.

**The Saltmaze (custom dungeon, BUILT):** maze-topology floors, Choir foes, guardians before
each stair, the Choirmaster at the bottom, and the blade.

**The blade (wielded, BUILT):** top-tier sword. **Sword beam at full HP** + **Wriftbane** —
bonus damage to corrupted creatures, elites/champions, and above all THE WRIFTHEART. The
blade that broke the heart bites deepest at what the break made. Never required to win; it
makes the final stretch sing.

**Tier note:** the blade's existence = Tier 1 (ballads). Who holds it = Tier 2. Where = Tier
2/3. That the Choir's god-relic being stolen by the Emberfall survivor is *poetry* = the
player's own discovery.

## 13. Working Notes

- **Shards-per-world (LOCKED):** every one of the 24 lands has a canonical shard (all named
  in the relic table), but each WORLD holds only **ten of them true** — the seed picks the
  ten lands (2 per tier 0–3, 1 each in tiers 4–5), and each holds the world's only dungeons.
  Canon cover: the ten shards sank into ten lands **and no two maps agree which** — every
  world/telling differs. Books must never assert which specific lands hold true shards.
- **One Spire per world (LOCKED):** mirror the shard rule — "no two maps agree where the Hum
  stands, only that it stands far." Books never give its bearing.
- Boss name styling: the creature is written **THE WRIFTHEART** in deep texts; casual books
  say "the thing in the castle" / "the Weeping King."
- Bald Hero ballads: write so they read as tall tale normally and prophecy if the hero is Baz.
- Never name the hero. Never date books after 312 A.S. Wren may be NAMED in Emberfall-region
  texts only; elsewhere she is "the one who stayed."
- The world is alive: books may note that beaten camps fill again and that the night and the
  seasons keep their own appointments (§2, §7) — always as observation, never as mechanics.

  write it in `src/lore_books.rs`. New micro-lore (whispers, tales, last words): add to the
  registry in §11 and keep the speaker's voice from §5.
